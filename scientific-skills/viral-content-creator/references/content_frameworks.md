# Content Frameworks for Social Media

> Proven structural frameworks adapted for short-form and social-native content creation.

---

## 1. AIDA for Short-Form Content

**Origin**: Classic advertising framework (Attention, Interest, Desire, Action).
**Adaptation**: Compressed for 60-second videos, carousel posts, and text posts.

### Structure

| Stage | Time/Space | Purpose | Tactics |
|-------|-----------|---------|---------|
| **Attention** (Hook) | First 1-3 seconds / first line | Stop the scroll | Bold claim, question, pattern interrupt, trending audio |
| **Interest** (Build) | Seconds 3-15 / slides 2-4 | Create investment | Unexpected insight, relatable problem, data point |
| **Desire** (Prove) | Seconds 15-45 / slides 5-8 | Make them want the outcome | Transformation, social proof, step-by-step |
| **Action** (CTA) | Final 5 seconds / last slide | Tell them exactly what to do | Follow, save, comment, click link, share |

### Platform Examples

**Instagram Carousel (10 slides)**:
- Slide 1: Hook headline (Attention)
- Slides 2-3: Problem amplification (Interest)
- Slides 4-7: Solution framework (Desire)
- Slide 8-9: Proof / results (Desire)
- Slide 10: CTA + branding (Action)

**TikTok Reel (45 seconds)**:
- 0-3s: Hook statement over trending audio (Attention)
- 3-15s: "Here's why this matters..." (Interest)
- 15-35s: Step-by-step demonstration (Desire)
- 35-45s: Result + "Follow for more" (Action)

**LinkedIn Text Post**:
- Line 1: Provocative opening (Attention)
- Lines 2-5: Context and stakes (Interest)
- Lines 6-15: Framework or story (Desire)
- Final lines: Question or CTA (Action)

## 2. StoryBrand Framework for Social Media

**Origin**: Donald Miller's StoryBrand framework.
**Core idea**: The customer is the hero. The brand is the guide. The content maps the journey from problem to success.

### The 7-Part Structure (Compressed)

1. **Character**: Your audience (not your brand) has a desire
2. **Problem**: External problem, internal frustration, philosophical injustice
3. **Guide**: Your brand appears with empathy + authority
4. **Plan**: Clear, simple steps to follow
5. **Call to Action**: Direct ("Buy now") or transitional ("Download the free guide")
6. **Success**: Paint the picture of life after the problem is solved
7. **Failure**: Hint at what happens if they don't act

### Social Media Adaptation

**Carousel version (8 slides)**:
- Slide 1: "You want [desire]. But [obstacle] keeps getting in the way." (Character + Problem)
- Slide 2: "We get it. We've been there." (Guide -- empathy)
- Slide 3: "After helping [number] [audience], we found [insight]." (Guide -- authority)
- Slides 4-6: "Here's the [number]-step plan." (Plan)
- Slide 7: "Imagine [success state]." / "Without this, [failure state]." (Success/Failure)
- Slide 8: "[CTA]" (Call to Action)

**Video version (60 seconds)**:
- 0-5s: "If you're a [character] trying to [desire]..." (Character)
- 5-15s: "...you probably deal with [problem]" (Problem -- external + internal)
- 15-20s: "We've helped [number] [people] solve this." (Guide)
- 20-45s: "Here's the plan: Step 1... Step 2... Step 3..." (Plan)
- 45-55s: "Now imagine [success]." (Success)
- 55-60s: "[CTA]" (Call to Action)

## 3. Hero's Journey in 60 Seconds

**Origin**: Joseph Campbell's monomyth, distilled for short-form video.
**Core idea**: Every compelling story follows a universal arc -- a character leaves the familiar, faces trials, transforms, and returns changed.

### Compressed Structure for Reels/TikTok

| Beat | Time | Content |
|------|------|---------|
| Ordinary World | 0-5s | "I used to be [relatable state]..." |
| Call to Adventure | 5-10s | "Then [event/realization] happened." |
| Trials | 10-25s | "I tried [failed approach 1], then [failed approach 2]." |
| The Revelation | 25-35s | "Until I discovered [key insight]." |
| Transformation | 35-50s | "Here's what changed: [specific actions/results]." |
| Return with the Gift | 50-60s | "And now I'm sharing it with you. [CTA]" |

### Adaptation Tips
- The "Ordinary World" must be deeply relatable to the target audience
- Failed approaches create authenticity (skip them and the story feels too clean)
- The revelation should be a specific, actionable insight -- not vague motivation
- The "gift" is the content itself -- the audience should feel they've received value

## 4. Hook-Story-Offer (HSO) Framework

**Origin**: Russell Brunson / direct response marketing.
**Core idea**: Every piece of content follows a three-part structure -- grab attention, build connection through narrative, then present the opportunity.

### Structure

**Hook** (10% of content):
- Bold claim, question, or pattern interrupt
- Must create enough curiosity to earn the next 10 seconds
- Speak directly to a felt pain or desired outcome

**Story** (70% of content):
- Personal narrative, case study, or audience story
- Must include struggle, turning point, and resolution
- Emotional resonance > logical argument
- Include specific details (numbers, dates, names) for credibility

**Offer** (20% of content):
- Not always a product -- can be "follow for more," "save this," or "try this method"
- Frame the offer as a natural extension of the story
- Remove friction: make the next step obvious and easy

### Platform Applications

**Twitter/X Thread**:
- Tweet 1: Hook (bold opening claim)
- Tweets 2-7: Story (narrative with specific details)
- Tweet 8: Offer (CTA -- follow, retweet, check link in bio)

**Instagram Reel**:
- 0-5s: Hook (face-to-camera, energy, bold statement)
- 5-45s: Story (narrated with b-roll or visual aids)
- 45-60s: Offer (what to do next, why it matters)

## 5. The 3-Act Instagram Carousel

**Origin**: Classical dramatic structure adapted for the swipe mechanic.
**Core idea**: Each "act" spans 3-4 slides, creating a beginning, middle, and end that respects the audience's declining attention across slides.

### Structure

**Act 1: The Setup (Slides 1-3)**
- Slide 1: The Hook -- bold statement, question, or promise
- Slide 2: The Context -- why this matters right now
- Slide 3: The Stakes -- what the audience stands to gain or lose

**Act 2: The Confrontation (Slides 4-7)**
- Slide 4: The first insight or step
- Slide 5: The deeper insight or complication
- Slide 6: The turning point or key revelation
- Slide 7: The proof (data, example, or testimonial)

**Act 3: The Resolution (Slides 8-10)**
- Slide 8: The synthesis -- connecting all insights
- Slide 9: The transformation -- what changes with this knowledge
- Slide 10: The CTA -- what to do next + branding

### Design Principles
- Each slide must earn the next swipe
- End each slide with an incomplete thought or transition word ("But...", "Here's the thing...", "And that leads to...")
- Maintain visual consistency but vary layouts to prevent monotony
- Slide 5-6 is the critical zone -- this is where most drop-offs happen. Make these slides the most visually striking.

## 6. The LinkedIn "Broetry" Structure

**Origin**: Emerged organically on LinkedIn circa 2019-2020. Short lines, heavy white space, emotionally driven narrative.
**Core idea**: Each line is a single thought. White space creates rhythm and readability on mobile. The format rewards scrolling.

### Structure Template

```
[Hook line -- bold, unexpected, personal]

[Short context line]
[Another short context line]

[Emotional or surprising turn]

[The lesson or insight -- 1 sentence]

[Supporting point 1]
[Supporting point 2]
[Supporting point 3]

[Synthesis -- connecting it all]

[CTA -- question or instruction]

---

[Hashtags -- 3-5 relevant ones]
```

### Example Pattern
```
I got fired on a Tuesday.

No warning.
No severance.
No plan B.

It was the best career move I never made.

Here's what unemployment taught me:

1. Your network is your net worth (cliche but real)
2. Skills compound; titles don't
3. The best job is the one you create

Three months later, I had more income than my salary.

What's the biggest career setback that turned into a breakthrough for you?
```

### Rules
- Max 1-2 sentences per paragraph
- No paragraph longer than 3 lines
- Hook must work in isolation (it's all that shows before "see more")
- Lists break up the narrative and add structure
- End with a genuine question (drives comments, which drive reach)
- Keep total length between 800-1,500 characters

## 7. The TikTok "Setup-Twist-Payoff" Structure

**Origin**: Comedy writing structure adapted for short-form video.
**Core idea**: Establish an expectation, subvert it, then deliver a satisfying resolution. This structure is the backbone of most viral TikToks.

### Structure

| Phase | Duration | Purpose |
|-------|----------|---------|
| **Setup** | 0-10s | Establish context, tone, and expectations |
| **Twist** | 10-20s | Subvert expectations with surprise, humor, or insight |
| **Payoff** | 20-30s+ | Deliver the resolution, lesson, or emotional peak |

### Variations

**Educational twist**:
- Setup: "Everyone says [common advice]"
- Twist: "But the data shows the opposite"
- Payoff: "Here's what actually works"

**Humor twist**:
- Setup: Normal-looking scenario
- Twist: Unexpected turn (visual or audio)
- Payoff: Punchline or reaction

**Emotional twist**:
- Setup: Seemingly ordinary story
- Twist: Reveal of deeper meaning or hidden struggle
- Payoff: Uplifting resolution or call to empathy

### Key Principles
- The setup must be specific enough to create a clear expectation
- The twist must genuinely surprise -- if the audience sees it coming, the format fails
- The payoff must satisfy the tension created by the twist
- Loop-friendly: if the payoff connects back to the setup, viewers will rewatch

## 8. The Content Flywheel Model

**Origin**: Jim Collins' flywheel concept applied to content marketing.
**Core idea**: Content creation is not linear. Each piece of content feeds the next in a self-reinforcing cycle.

### The Cycle

```
CREATE (produce content)
    |
    v
DISTRIBUTE (publish across platforms)
    |
    v
ENGAGE (interact with audience response)
    |
    v
LEARN (analyze what worked and why)
    |
    v
CREATE (produce better content, informed by data)
```

### Practical Application

**Create**: Use the content brief schema to produce platform-native content.

**Distribute**: Adapt one core idea across multiple platforms and formats. A single insight becomes:
- A LinkedIn text post
- An Instagram carousel
- A TikTok video
- A Twitter/X thread
- A YouTube Short
- A newsletter section

**Engage**: Respond to every comment in the first hour. Comments on comments create threads that boost algorithmic distribution. Note which topics generate the most discussion.

**Learn**: Track three metrics per post:
1. **Reach metric** (impressions, views)
2. **Engagement metric** (saves, shares, comments)
3. **Conversion metric** (profile visits, follows, link clicks)

Identify patterns: Which hooks work? Which formats? Which topics? Which posting times?

**Create again**: Feed insights back into the next content cycle. Double down on what works. Experiment with what might.

### Flywheel Acceleration
The flywheel is slow at first. The first 30 days feel unrewarding. By day 90, compounding effects become visible. By day 180, the flywheel has its own momentum.

Key accelerators:
- **Repurposing**: One idea becomes 5+ pieces of content
- **Series**: Returning audiences create reliable baseline engagement
- **Collaboration**: Cross-pollinating audiences adds energy to the wheel
- **Audience participation**: UGC and community content reduce creation load while increasing engagement

## Framework Selection Guide

| Situation | Best Framework | Why |
|-----------|---------------|-----|
| Selling a product/service | StoryBrand | Positions audience as hero, brand as guide |
| Educating the audience | AIDA | Structured attention-to-action pipeline |
| Personal branding | Hero's Journey | Vulnerability and transformation build trust |
| Quick engagement | Setup-Twist-Payoff | Surprise drives shares and replays |
| Thought leadership | LinkedIn Broetry | Authority + accessibility in text format |
| Deep-dive content | 3-Act Carousel | Sustained attention across multiple slides |
| Community building | Content Flywheel | Systematic engagement loop |
| Conversion-focused | Hook-Story-Offer | Direct path from attention to action |

## Combining Frameworks

Frameworks can be nested. A content calendar (Flywheel) contains individual posts that use AIDA. A carousel (3-Act) contains slides that follow StoryBrand. A TikTok (Setup-Twist-Payoff) can be rewritten as a LinkedIn post (Broetry) using the same core insight.

The framework is the skeleton. The brand voice, topic expertise, and audience understanding are the muscle.
